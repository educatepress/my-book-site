## What is Preconception Care? Why is Folic Acid So Critical?

Preconception care is a form of "health preparation" before pregnancy, aiming not only for a healthy baby but also for the mother's sustained health. There are two primary reasons why folic acid is indispensable in this preparation.

### The Basics of Folic Acid: A Key Player in Cell Division and DNA Synthesis

Folic acid, a B vitamin, is a vital nutrient deeply involved in cell division, proliferation, and DNA synthesis. During pregnancy, a fetus's cells grow at an astonishing rate within the mother's body, requiring even more folic acid than usual.

### Essential for Reducing the Risk of Neural Tube Defects (NTDs)

Folic acid's prominence in preconception care stems from its effectiveness in reducing the risk of Neural Tube Defects (NTDs) in babies.

Neural tubes, which develop into the brain and spinal cord, form incredibly early – within just one month after conception, specifically around weeks 4 to 7 of pregnancy. In many cases, this development is complete before a woman even realizes she's pregnant. Therefore, if you start taking folic acid only after confirming pregnancy, it might be too late. This is why it's critically important to start folic acid intake from the planning stage of pregnancy.

## Recommended Folic Acid Intake and Timing

So, how much folic acid should you take, and when should you start?

### Recommended Intake by MHLW and WHO

Public health bodies such as Japan's Ministry of Health, Labour and Welfare (MHLW), the World Health Organization (WHO), and the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommend that women planning pregnancy take 400 micrograms (mcg) of supplemental folic acid daily, in addition to their regular diet, to reduce the risk of NTDs. This is the amount advised to be supplemented, separate from dietary intake.

### Ideal Intake Timing

The most effective timing for intake is to begin folic acid supplementation at least one month before planning conception, ideally 2 to 3 months prior, and continue throughout the first trimester of pregnancy (up to around week 12). This ensures that sufficient folic acid is accumulated in the body during the critical period when the neural tube is forming.

## Food Sources vs. Supplements: The Role of Each

While folic acid is found in various foods, meeting the necessary amount solely through diet to reduce the risk of NTDs can be challenging.

### Foods Rich in Folic Acid

*   Leafy Green Vegetables: Spinach, broccoli, asparagus, edamame, etc.
*   Legumes: Soybeans, natto (fermented soybeans), lentils, etc.
*   Liver: Chicken liver, pork liver, etc. (Note: Pregnant women should be cautious with liver intake due to potential vitamin A overdose).
*   Fruits: Strawberries, avocado, etc.

However, folic acid in food is water-soluble and heat-sensitive, meaning it can be lost during cooking. Furthermore, folic acid found in foods (polyglutamate form) has a lower absorption rate compared to folic acid found in supplements (monoglutamate form).

### Supplementation is the Most Recommended and Reliable Method

Therefore, for the purpose of reducing the risk of neural tube defects, combining folic acid supplements is the most reliable and strongly recommended method. Supplements allow you to consistently take the recommended amount daily, and their higher absorption rate efficiently increases folic acid concentration in the body.

## Beyond Folic Acid: Comprehensive Preconception Care

Folic acid intake is just the beginning of preconception care. For a healthy pregnancy and delivery, many other factors should be considered:

*   Healthy Diet: A balanced diet, paying attention to other nutrients as needed.
*   Regular Exercise: Maintain a healthy weight and reduce stress.
*   Smoking Cessation and Moderate Alcohol Intake: If you're planning pregnancy, limit or avoid these as much as possible.
*   Weight Management: Being underweight or overweight can affect pregnancy.
*   Management of Chronic Conditions: If you have conditions like diabetes or thyroid disease, consult your doctor in advance.
*   Stress Reduction: Make time to relax both mentally and physically.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

### Q1: How long should I continue taking folic acid?

A1: It's recommended to continue taking folic acid at least through the first trimester (up to around week 12 of pregnancy). This period is most crucial for reducing the risk of neural tube defects. Continuing throughout the mid and late stages of pregnancy can also help maintain maternal health, such as preventing anemia.

### Q2: Are folic acid supplements truly necessary?

A2: For the purpose of reducing the risk of neural tube defects, it is strongly recommended to take supplements, as it can be difficult to obtain a sufficient amount from diet alone. For women who are planning to conceive or may become pregnant, supplements play an essential, almost critical, role.
